Welcome to the GoodWin Project!

We are a group of data science students, and are all passionate about conserving biodiversity around the world. We want to share our findings and analysis to increase knowledge of conservation and inspire action.


Why "GoodWin"?


The two conservationists that inspire us the most are Jane Goodall and Steve Irwin. Good + win = GoodWin.

Jane Goodall

Jane Goodall is the world's leading expert on chimpanzees. She is the founder of the Jane Goodall Institute, an organization that raises awareness and implements protection for chimpanzees and species all around the world.


Steve Irwin

Steve Irwin was most widely known as being "The Crocodile Hunter". He was also one of the biggest conservation activists until his death in 2006. His legacy lives on and is championed by the Australia Zoo.




What makes our project unique?


Animal population data is hard to come by. It's not easy to access or understand. It is often incomplete, silo-ed, and messy. And, there are a little-to-no future population predictions. Until now, qualitative risk identification for single species at a time was the only way of determining the threat level of that species becoming endangered. We sought to create a one-stop-shop with historical populations and forecasted populations calculated from our time series models.

To examine an individual species' past and predicted populations, start by selecting a country on the map, and then a species from the generated list. The populations will then appear on the bottom graph. A few things to keep in mind:

- The data have varying units of measurement
- The data are not uniform across time (certain species will have longer or more consistent periods of observation than others)
- We project each species population through 2025 so the information is relevant today

Data

While we can glean great insights from population information for species with well recorded data, there are many other species where this is not possible. Though usually lack of data is a dead end for many projects, in relevance to animal conservation, lack of data for species is an important insight on its own, and a potentially crucial one too. There are a few reasons why there is a lack of data for certain species:

  • No funding for some years
  • Inability to measure population in certain locations
  • Organizations don’t want to publicize information about a threatened and hunted species
  • Simply no one doing research on that species
  • Maybe that species wasn’t discovered until recently


One of the greatest challenges in animal conservation is not knowing what we don’t know. Our project has helped identify some of that into the things we know that we don’t know category! But identification of these species and their lack of population data sheds light to the areas of conservation that have yet to be explored and give upcoming conservationists new adventures to tackle.
